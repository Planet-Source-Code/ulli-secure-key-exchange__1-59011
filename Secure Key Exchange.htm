Secure Key Exchange

developed at Stanford University by Whitfield Diffie, Martin Hellman and Ralph Merkle

 

How do you exchange a secret key between two parties over an unsecure connection? Well, you couldn’t. Up to the 70’s key exchange required personal meetings or a trustworthy messenger. With the advent of the internet however the requirement for a secure key exchange over unsecure lines became evident. The team at Stanford soon found that a one-way operation (or a trapdoor function as the named it) was needed. A trapdoor function is one that is quick an easy one way but to invert it is near-impossible without the knowledge of a missing piece of information.

 

An example might be a pseudo-random number generator: You seed it and it generates a determinate series of numbers, but if you have one single number out of the series (say the nth number) it is impossible to determine what the seed was. The missing piece of information in this case is n.

 

Modulo arithmetic also leads to one-way operations, and so the Stanford team was very interested in modulo arithmetic. They finally settled on a modulo operation of the form:

 

p ^ xn mod q

 

Using this operation, they were able to come up with a secret key exchange algorithm which works as follows:

 

Step 1:

Alice and Bob agree on two non-secret values, one for the variable p in the formula above, the other for the variable q, and p must be less than q. For example, they agree on p = 6 and q = 17 .

 

Step 2:

Then Alice and Bob each choose a secret value, xa and xb, let’s say Alice chooses xa = 7 and Bob chooses xb = 5.

 

Step 3:

Alice uses her secret value xa = 7 in the formula defined above:

 

 (p ^ xa) mod q = (6 ^ 7) mod 17 = 279,936 mod 17 and gets a new value pa = 14

 

She sends that value to Bob.

 

Step 4:

Bob similarly inserts his secret value xb = 5 into the formula:

 

 (p ^ xb) mod q = (6 ^ 5) mod 17 = 7,776 mod 17 = pb = 7

 

He sends this result to Alice.

 

Step 5:

Now here's where the really surprising thing happens. Alice takes Bob's result of pb= 7 and plugs it into the formula as:

 

 (pb ^ xa) mod q = (7 ^ 7) mod 17 = 823,543 mod 17 = 12

 

Step 6:

Likewise, Bob takes Alice's result of pa = 14 and sticks it into the formula:

 

 (pa ^ xb) mod q = (14 ^ 5 ) mod 17 = 537,824 mod 17 = 12

 

The results are the same for Alice and Bob. Alice and Bob will always get the same value if they follow this procedure, and that value is the cipher key, in this case with a value of 12. Proving that this neat symmetry is true is a job for the professionals, but the rest of us can take it as granted that it is so.

 

Of course, 12 is too feeble a value for a secret key, and in practice Alice and Bob would pick much bigger values for p and q to obtain a stronger cipher key. However, the calculations quickly become burdensome and a simple example as demonstrated here must use small values to keep calculations manageable. They still often overflow the resolution of a pocket calculator and generally require an indefinite-precision math software package.

 

Now suppose an eavesdropper (named Eve for convenience) has been listening in and has intercepted everything that was exchanged between Alice and Bob: p, q, pa,  and pb.

 

The important feature of this scheme is that Eve will still not be able to figure out what the key is. She has to know the secret values of xa and xb. Alice hasn't told anyone what the value of xa is, and Bob hasn't told anyone what the value of xb is. They don't have to tell anyone, not even each other, and so in this scenario there is no opportunity for Eve to intercept their secret values xa  and xb to determine the key agreed upon between Alice and Bob.

 

However, the Diffie-Hellman key exchange is still vulnerable to a person-in-the-middle attack. In this attack, Eve, knowing p and q already, intercepts Alice's public value pa and sends her own public value to Bob. When Bob transmits his public value pb, Eve substitutes it with her own and sends it to Alice. Alice and Eve thus agree on one shared key and Eve and Bob agree on another shared key. After this exchange, Eve simply decrypts any messages sent out by Alice to Bob or vice versa, and then reads and possibly modifies them before re-encrypting with the appropriate key and transmitting them to the other party. This vulnerability is present because the Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange does not authenticate the participants.

 

One solution for intimacy and integrity might be the following scheme using two private, non-disclosed keys alpha and beta:

 

Alice encrypts her message using her private key α and sends it to Bob. Bob encrypts the encrypted message again using his private key β and sends this now doubly encrypted message back to Alice. Alice decrypts using her private key (thus removing her α-encryption, leaving only Bob’s β-encryption) and returns the message to Bob whereupon Bob can finally decrypt the message with his own private key. This procedure of course requires a special type of symmetrical encryption which is not upset by the second encryption pass:

 

((((M enc α  enc β) dec α) dec β) = M

 

Since all messages sent to and fro between Alice and Bob have at least been encrypted once (using a private non-disclosed key) there is virtually no chance for Eve to figure out what the message content is.
